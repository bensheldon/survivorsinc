Unity

Our Winter 2007 issue of Survival News is about unity. We are living in a time when people are pitted against each other: immigrant vs. native- born, wage-earner vs. welfare recipient, communities vs. ex-prisoners, Americans vs. Iraqis. We believe that a clear analysis and concrete work are necessary to forge bonds across these unacceptable boundaries, so that we may unite to work for our common interests. It is in that spirit thatwededicate the stories in these pages.—The Editors

HOUSING IS A HUMAN RIGHT!

Article 25 of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights

1. Everyone has the right to a standard of living adequate for the health and well-being of her/himself and of her/his family, including food, clothing, HOUSING and medical care and necessary social services, and the right to security in the event of unemployment, sickness, disability, widowhood, old age or other lack of livelihood in circumstances beyond her/his control.

2. Motherhood and childhood are entitled to special care and assistance. All children, whether born in or out of wedlock, shall enjoy the same social protection.

Section 8 Housing Cuts – No More War on the Poor!

By Julie Joy

I have Section 8 housing. When I first heard last year that people would be losing Section 8 vouchers, I was terrified. When I read that it would be "last in-first out", I was, at first, relieved because I thought, "that won't hurt me". Then I realized that if any of us aren't safe, none of us are safe. I spoke out at a hearing at the State House with the DHCD. I spoke at rallies and demonstrations. Now they’re debating lifetime limits for all Section 8 housing. We have to speak out on behalf of those who can’t. Surviving poverty takes a lot of work and many can't possibly be activists in addition to spending every waking minute trying to keep their families safe and alive.

I don't like to use the word "housing". It is not descriptive of my reality. This is not just about housing or shelter to me. It's my home. It's like when my landlord uses the word, "property", it makes me shudder. It's not about property for me. It's not about investing in the real estate market. We're talking about my home. It's the place that is security and safety and warmth and special things and the place where my daughters have their childhood memories. My six-year-old plays in every corner of our home. We have family dinners and lots of holidays. We rush in from the rain and yell; "It's so good to be home!" This is not something that some legislator who is sitting in the security of their untouchable private homes should have the authority to rip away from my family and I. We're not pawns in some financial balancing act. These are our lives and the lives of our children, our neighbors, our friends, our co-workers and our mothers. You can't just relocate us or throw us out into the street. For some, our very survival will depend on it. We will die from some consequence of these actions. It is murder. They're killing us.

Be clear about one thing…the money is there. There is no magic needed to come up with the money to fund the Section 8 program. It would just take a slight shift in consciousness. It is only a matter of priorities. The government has chosen to capitalize on the public's fears by scaring us about potential terrorism. So, the government has made a decision to spend billions of dollars for "homeland security" while at the same time taking our homes and our security away from us, the poorest people in America. So I am terrified right now. The danger I am facing is imminent. My home, my health, my safety, my children's education and well being, perhaps even our lives are in jeopardy. Creating homeless families is a brazen act of terrorism on the part of the government. We know it's true because it's our lives.

So I say to you, HUD, DHCD, MBHP and all other housing agency staff…above all else, do no harm. You can’t simultaneously fight homelessness and create homelessness. Do not be an instrument in these atrocities. Refuse to cut even one person from the Section 8 program. Make a stand. Ask other agencies to make a stand. Don't have hearings to ask us to help you decide the fairest way to take our homes away. There is no fairness in any approach. Do not pit us against each other. Home is where our hearts are. Have a heart and don't proceed with this plan. Instead, put all of your available resources into fighting the cuts. Put your jobs on the line rather than putting our lives on the line. You have a chance here to take a stand, to make a real difference in people's lives, to protect the people you serve. You have more power than you think and we collectively have the power to change these cuts. Don't back down. Refuse to be an instrument of this war on the poor. Our lives depend on it.

"The Poverty Truth Commission"

On Friday, April 15, 2005, the community at Union Theological Seminary was asked to confront the words of Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr., "There comes a time when silence is betrayal. The truth must be told." This community on April 15th, however, was not merely the traditional Union community. For the day, it was an extended community of poor people, activists, church leaders, social leaders, and all those who wished to engage in a conversation about the growing economic injustices that are taking place in our country.

The day began with a multi-faith prayer service where people from different faith traditions came together to offer their prayers and reflections for the eradication of poverty. We were asked to come on behalf of our sisters and brothers who could not lift up their own voices and to demand a vision of the world in which everyone is valued by our society and poverty is known no more.

After the prayer service, participants attended a series of workshops led by poor people’s organizations, including Picture the Homeless, the Tompkins County Living Wage Coalition, Poor People United, the Abyssinian Development Corporation, and Broadway Community Inc. The workshops were keyed to the United Nations Universal Declaration of Human Rights which addresses economic human rights to food, housing, healthcare, and a living wage. A documentary viewing and discussion of The Philadelphia Story, led by members of the Kensington Welfare Rights Union and the Poor People’s Economic Human Rights Campaign, closed the daytime session.

The day-long event culminated with the Poverty Truth Commission, whose goal was to create a space where the truth could be told. That night no one was silent: from the stomping feet of the Send Judah First Step Team from The Riverside Church to the fervent voices of We Shall Overcome, the walls of Union’s James Chapel echoed with cries for justice. Ronald Casanova (Artists for a Better America), Carolyn Brown (Poor Peoples United), Sunshine Bannister (Tompkins County Living Wage Coalition) gave personal testimonies that laid bare the realities of poverty and challenged the community to join the fight to end it. And as the night came to a close, the petitions of the testifiers were met with bold commitments from the commission of prominent religious, academic, and community leaders including The Rev. Dr. Robert W. Edgar (National Council of Churches U.S.A.), The Rev. Dr. James A. Forbes, Jr. (The Riverside Church), and Union’s own President, The Rev. Dr. Joseph C. Hough, who proclaimed that no Union Student would graduate without directly encountering the tangible realities of poverty. Cheri Honkala (Poor People’s Economic Human Rights Campaign) left us with the understanding that while this fight will not be easy, we have made a commitment to join a movement that is calling for the elimination of poverty – not simply reducing, alleviating, or managing it.

The commitment to join the poor in their fight to eliminate poverty is a goal that the Poverty Initiative at Union Theological Seminary has placed at the heart of its work. We believe it is imperative that people of faith work together toward liberation of all of God’s children from economic injustices. We have committed ourselves to transcending the divides of religion, culture, race, and class by working to bring people together to embrace this fight to end poverty. For more information about the Poverty Initiative at Union Theological Seminary visit povertyinitiative.org.
